How to write the factual information summary?

(100 Word summaries)
The summaries for your outputs are very important. They are meant to demonstrate each output's significance and give the REF panel an indication of "factual information about the research questions, methodology or means of dissemination... not described in the output itself". This information will likely have a significant impact on the review of the output so it's important you follow the guidance. Also, keep the summary brief. The REF guidance suggests "in most cases, sufficient information will be provided in significantly fewer words than the 100-word limit".

Examples:

The University of Edinburgh have a helpful page with examples here.
I've collated additional examples here.

Include information on:

  • Impact on state of art, e.g. best paper awards, related keynotes, cited as seminal work by important people/institutions. Hint: as a start, use the Dimensions badge to check citations.
  • Further developments, e.g. taken up in subsequent research, especially by leaders in the field, or used as a basis for a major grant or partnership.
  • External recognition, e.g. used in commercial contexts, informed patents, used by startups, or used in wider societal impact or public policy.

Do not include:

  • A synopsis. This is not meant to be an abstract.
  • Citation data. This is provided to the REF panel, independently, likely using Web of Science metadata.
  • Opinions of the quality of the output. This is for the sub-panel to do.
  • Non verifiable information. You may need to provide information on how to verufy any claims in the 100 words.

Writing suggestions:

  • Focus a non-expert on the work's contribution and significance.
  • To structure the information: begin by outlining the context for the work in a single sentence. The second sentence should then start with “This is significant because”, or something similar.
  • Be succinct, and make factual points that are verifiable (using external references where appropriate).
  • Compare the contribution to other work in your area if it highlights originality, rigour or significance.
  • In most cases, sufficient information can be provided in significantly fewer words than the 100 word limit.

How will outputs be judged?

Outputs will be judged against a quality profile:

  • Four Star: Quality that is world-leading in terms of originality, significance and rigour.
  • Three Star: Quality that is internationally excellent in terms of originality, significance and rigour but which falls short of the highest standards of excellence.
  • Two Star: Quality that is recognised internationally in terms of originality, significance and rigour.
  • One Star: Quality that is recognised nationally in terms of originality, significance and rigour.
  • Unclassified: Quality that falls below the standard of nationally recognised work. Or work which does not meet the published definition of research for the purposes of this assessment.

Originality, Significance and Rigour

The following will be used to determine the quality profile and start rating:

  • Originality will be understood as the extent to which the output introduces a new way of thinking about a subject, or is distinctive or transformative compared with previous work in an academic field.
  • Significance will be understood as the extent to which the work has exerted, or is likely to exert, an influence on an academic field or practical applications.
  • Rigour will be understood as the extent to which the purpose of the work is clearly articulated, an appropriate methodology for the research area has been adopted, and compelling evidence presented to show that the purpose has been achieved.
