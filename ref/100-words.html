Factual information in 100 words

The following offers some recommendations for writing the 100 word 'factual information' for REF2021 outputs. This information has been taken and synthesised from other resources available online.

REF Guidance REF Panel criteria

About 100 words

One-hundred word summaries will be used by REF 2021 Computer Science, sub-panel 11, to make quick assessments of individual outputs. Hopefully panelists will look at each output in full, but these summaries will play a very significant role.

For further information from REF see the REF Guidance (paragraph 284) and specific REF 11 Panel criteria (paragraphs 256 and 257).


Appended are:

  • Some do and donts.
  • A list of the criteria authors should keep in mind in writing their 100 words, and specifically summaries of Originality, Significance and Rigour.
  • Some examples.

Does

  • Present the contribution of the work to a non-expert in your area. Show originality, rigour and significance (with a balance between these three that is suited to the output but doesn't duplicate what is in it). Significance may be the hardest but most important thing to capture.
  • Show evidence of the value of the work that is not in the paper. For example, if it was given a best paper award; presented as a keynote; presented "unbeaten" results; cited as seminal work by important people/institutions; had impact on other’s work, follow on work, grant applications, patents, etc.
  • To structure the information: begin by outlining the context for the work in a single sentence. The second sentence should then start with “This is significant because”, or something similar.
  • Be succinct, and make factual points that are verifiable (using external references where appropriate).
  • Compare the contribution to other work in your area if it highlights originality, rigour or significance.
  • In most cases, sufficient information can be provided in significantly fewer words than the 100 word limit.

Donts

  • Don't write a synopsis of the output.
  • Don't Make a case for the quality of the output. This is for the sub-panel to do.
  • Don't refer to citation data or impact factors.


Originality, Significance and Rigour of outputs

Originality will be understood as the extent to which the output introduces a new way of thinking about a subject, or is distinctive or transformative compared with previous work in an academic field.

Significance will be understood as the extent to which the work has exerted, or is likely to exert, an influence on an academic field or practical applications.

Rigour will be understood as the extent to which the purpose of the work is clearly articulated, an appropriate methodology for the research area has been adopted, and compelling evidence presented to show that the purpose has been achieved.


Examples

The University of Edinburgh have a helpful page with examples here.

"Early version of work appears in ICCV 2003 (acceptance rate 23%). Idea taken further by Gori and co-workers in 2005 IEEE PAMI paper. Robles-Kelly RA/RS supervised by Hancock. Work supported by EPSRC project (Spectral Retrieval from Large Structural Databases); final report rated internationally leading/outstanding."

"Early versions of work appear in ICCV 2005n(acceptance rate 20%) and as a podium presentation at CVPR 2006 (5% accaptance rate). Work has proved influential on Ikeuchi group at Tokyo U (PAMI 29/11). Work potentially points to the design of a non-invasive range scanner that can be used for untextured and structureless surfaces and for determining material reflectance properties."

"Knudsen, one of the world's leading cryptographers, cited this paper (and two others) in his position paper to the EU STORK consortium, stating: "It is of the utmost importance that the new tools are studied extensively..." (www.stork.eu.org/papers/19b_stork_lars.pdf)."

"This paper was shortlisted for the "Best of CHI 06" award, meaning it was in the top 5% of papers accepted for the main conference in HCI, with an acceptance rate of 23%."

"Presented at CHI 07, the main conference in HCI, with an acceptance rate of 23%."

"This journal paper is a revised and extended version of a paper presented at the GECCO 2005 international conference, which won a 2005 Human Competitive Awards ("Hummies") $1000 silver medal. The Hummies are the world leading award in Genetic Programming (GP). The paper describes the use of GP to evolve a general-purpose size-independent quantum algorithm to implement Shor's Quantum Fourier Transform, the basis of Shor's famous polynomial time factoring algorithm. This work demonstrates, for the first time, that automated computer-based techniques can be used to evolve significant quantum computing artefacts."

"The work in this paper has led to 2 patent applications (reference Simon Forrest of NCR). This is the first major industrial application of AIS and will has a major impact on machine availability. Timmis was the lead researcher in the project."

"The work was used as the main input to a successful EU STREP project proposal (Top rated) called HIJA IST-511718, and is one of the main inputs into the current international attempts to define a standard for Safety Critical Java (Java Community Process: Experts Group for JSR 302)."

"Workflow provenance captures information about an execution of a workflow, and queries over provenance data are widely used to clarify how the results of a workflow were obtained. This paper is significant because previous work on querying workflow provenance traversed the provenance data, whereas this approach only traverses the (much smaller) workflow definition, making relatively few and simple queries into the provenance data itself; an empirical evaluation reveals significant performance benefits. Included in public releases of the widely used Taverna workflow system."

"The paper experimentally compares published load balancing techniques for parallel database query processing, identifies weaknesses in those techniques, and proposes a new method that addresses these weaknesses. This is significant because previously adaptive query load balancing techniques had only been compared with non- adaptive techniques and not with each other, and because the systematic evaluation in the paper provided new insights, for example by finding cases where adaptations reduced performance."

"Techniques that support or automate the annotation of web services are important because such annotations are useful for service discovery and composition, but are tricky and time consuming to author manually. This paper is significant in supporting automatic derivation of dependable annotations, showing for the first time how workflow provenance can be used to propagate annotations between services. The empirical evaluation shows how the approach identified previously unidentified errors with manual annotations of e-Science services."
